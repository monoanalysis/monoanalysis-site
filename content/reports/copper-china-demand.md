## Motivation

China accounts for over 50% of global refined copper consumption. The country's property sector slowdown since 2022 has raised persistent questions about demand destruction, yet copper prices have remained resilient. This report investigates whether alternative demand drivers — particularly grid infrastructure and EV manufacturing — are offsetting the property drag.

## Data & Methodology

We track three high-frequency demand proxies: National Bureau of Statistics monthly property starts (floor space), State Grid Corporation capital expenditure announcements, and the Caixin Manufacturing PMI new orders subcomponent. These are mapped against LME copper spot prices, SHFE warehouse stocks, and CFTC managed money positioning.

The analysis covers January 2023 through March 2026 using weekly and monthly data points. We compute rolling correlations between each demand proxy and subsequent 30-day copper price moves to identify which indicators carry the most predictive signal.

## Results

Property starts have declined 38% from their 2021 peak but appear to have stabilized in late 2025. The correlation between property starts and copper prices has weakened from 0.62 (2018-2021) to 0.28 (2023-2026), suggesting the market has partially de-linked from the housing cycle.

Grid infrastructure spending has become the dominant demand driver, with State Grid capex up 22% year-over-year in 2025. The correlation between grid investment announcements and subsequent copper price moves is 0.51 over the study period.

SHFE warehouse stocks remain at historically low levels, indicating tight physical availability despite macro headwinds. The SHFE-LME arbitrage window has been intermittently open, pulling imported copper into China.

## Interpretation

The copper market is undergoing a structural demand rotation in China. Portfolio managers and physical traders who rely on property data alone are using an increasingly stale demand model. Grid infrastructure and electrification capex should be weighted more heavily in fundamental copper models. The tight warehouse stock situation suggests that any demand surprise to the upside could trigger a sharp price response given limited available inventory.
