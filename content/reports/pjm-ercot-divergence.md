## Motivation

The US operates two fundamentally different power market designs side by side. PJM pays generators for availability through capacity auctions. ERCOT relies solely on energy and ancillary service prices to incentivize investment. The 2021 Winter Storm Uri reignited debate over which design better ensures reliability.

## Data & Methodology

We compare 2018-2026 data on capacity auction clearing prices (PJM RPM), real-time energy price distributions (both markets), new generation interconnection queues, and reliability metrics (LOLE, EUE). Investment signals are measured by the implied return on a new gas peaker in each market.

## Results

PJM capacity revenues provide roughly $45-65/kW-year in stable revenue to generators, reducing energy price volatility but also suppressing scarcity pricing signals. ERCOT's energy-only prices produce higher peak revenues but greater variance. New generation investment in ERCOT has outpaced PJM since 2022, driven primarily by renewables and battery storage responding to high peak prices.

## Interpretation

Neither design is strictly superior. PJM offers revenue stability that supports baseload and dispatchable generation. ERCOT rewards flexibility and fast-response assets. The convergence of both markets toward higher renewable penetration may eventually force hybrid approaches.
